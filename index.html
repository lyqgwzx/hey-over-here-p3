“Hey, Over Here!”

A voice-summoned mobile waste-collection robot for shared offices — studied as a browser simulation.

Li Ya (李亚)  ·  AP0011 Human-Computer Interaction  ·  Instructor: Sahba Zojaji  ·  SUSTech / SLAI  ·  Final Project (P3)

Abstract

Summoning an in-office service robot today usually means opening an app and tapping through menus. This project asks whether summoning by voice can replace that. The robot responds to a spoken “over here”, estimates the caller’s direction by sound (sound-source localization), and matches it to a named workstation. We rebuild the scenario as a browser simulation and run a within-subjects study across three ways to summon — app, voice only, and voice with visual feedback — measuring effort, trust (especially when the robot mislocates the caller), multi-user fairness, and cognitive load.

How it works

  1. You say “over here” from your desk — no app, no buttons.
  2. A microphone array estimates the direction your voice came from (sound-source localization).
  3. That direction is matched to a named workstation — it knows “that’s Ben’s desk”, not just an angle.
  4. The robot drives over and collects the waste.

Three ways to summon — what we compare

App (baseline). Open an app, pick your desk, confirm — several taps.

Voice only. Say “over here”. The robot finds you by sound, but gives no feedback, so you can’t tell what it heard until it arrives.

Voice + visual. Same voice, but the robot shows what it understood — a light, a beam toward where it thinks you are, the desk it’s heading to, and an ETA.

The key question: sound-source localization sometimes sends the robot to the wrong desk. Does the visual feedback let people keep trusting it appropriately when that happens?

Two ways to experience it

3D · first-person (recommended). You’re seated at your own desk and can’t see the whole office — the robot may be behind you or hidden by a partition. It makes a spatially-positioned sound, so you can locate it by ear (use headphones). This is the rigorous build: because you can’t watch the whole room, the “no-feedback” condition is genuinely low-information — which is exactly what the trust comparison needs.

2D · top-down. A quick, any-device overhead view of the same study — handy as a baseline and on phones without headphones. Caveat: the bird’s-eye view lets you watch the robot the whole time, so it understates the value of visual feedback (a known limitation the 3D build fixes).

What it measures

  • Interaction friction — taps and time to arrival.
  • Calibrated trust — whether feedback helps you judge the robot, especially when it goes to the wrong desk.
  • Multi-user fairness — how it handles several people summoning at once.
  • Cognitive load — how demanding each method feels (NASA-TLX).
